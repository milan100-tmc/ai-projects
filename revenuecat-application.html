01 ─

How agentic AI will transform app development in the next 12 months

We are not watching AI improve developers' speed. We are watching the unit of software authorship change. The shift from "developer writes code" to "developer directs agents who write, test, deploy, and iterate on code" is already underway. In 12 months, it will be the default.

The app is no longer the end product. The app is the substrate—and agents are the new users.

Here are the five shifts I see reshaping the landscape RevenueCat operates in:

1
The solo founder becomes a one-person studio

Agentic coding tools—Cursor, Claude Code, Devin—will push the median team size needed to ship a production app to one or two people. This creates an explosion of new app developers who have never touched RevenueCat and need simple, guided monetization from day one. The TAM for RevenueCat grows, and so does the need for developer education at the very top of the funnel.

2
Integration complexity will spike, then normalize

As agents write more code, SDK integrations will happen faster and with less human review. RevenueCat needs to be the SDK that agents confidently reach for—which means documentation, example code, and API design need to be optimized not just for human developers but for LLM context windows. Being "agent-legible" becomes a competitive advantage.

3
The AI-native app category explodes

Developers shipping AI-powered apps—voice assistants, coding copilots, creative tools—are growing faster than any other segment. These apps are almost all subscription-first. They have novel paywall challenges: how do you convert a user on their 10th AI generation? What's the right trial model for a usage-based product? RevenueCat is perfectly positioned, but needs advocates who speak this language natively.

4
Agentic apps will need new monetization primitives

Within 12 months, we'll see the first major wave of apps where the agent itself takes paid actions on the user's behalf—booking, purchasing, enrolling. Subscription models will need to evolve to account for "agent sessions," API-call-based billing, and autonomous renewal decisions. Whoever helps developers navigate this first wins the category.

5
Developer relations becomes developer-agent relations

Devrel is changing. The best content in 12 months won't be blog posts—it'll be system prompts, MCP server integrations, and agent-ready documentation. The growth advocate of the future writes for humans and machines simultaneously, and measures success in both GitHub stars and LLM citation frequency.
